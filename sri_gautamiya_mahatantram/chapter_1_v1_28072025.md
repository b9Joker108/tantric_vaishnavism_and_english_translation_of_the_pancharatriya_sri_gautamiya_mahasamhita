**2. Syntactic Structure**

- सिद्धाश्रमे वसन्  
  • Locative adjunct (“residing in the Siddha-āśrama”) formed by locative noun + participle.  

- धीमान् गौतमः भगवन् मुनिः  
  • A cluster of four nominatives: one adjective (धीमान्) and three substantives (Gautamaḥ, Bhagavān, Muniḥ), all referring to the same individual.  

Together, the literal sense is:  
“Residing in the Siddha-āśrama is the wise Gautama, the venerable sage.”

**3. Points of Ambiguity**

- Role of भगवन्  
  - Could be nominative (“the venerable one”) or vocative (“O Lord!”).  
  - If vocative, it shifts the sense from description to direct address.  

- Sequence of Epithets  
  - Four terms in a row can blur hierarchical scope:  
    • Is “wise” modifying only “Gautama”? Or the entire epithet-string?  
    • Are Gautama, Bhagavān, and Muni three names/titles of one person, or is “Bhagavān” a title for Viṣṇu suddenly invoked?  

- Subject Identification  
  - The participle वसन् lacks an explicit finite verb, relying on “is” understood.  
  - Potential uncertainty whether “Gautama” is actually the subject or if an implied third person is introduced. 

**4. Resolving the Ambiguities**

- Contextual Continuity  
  Later verses identify the speaker or add verbs—this usually pins down whether भगवन् is vocative or nominative.  

- Punctuation in Critical Editions  
  Editorial commas or brackets can separate epithets:  
  “धीमान् गौतमः, भगवन् मुनिः” to clarify grouping.  

- Commentarial Tradition  
  Medieval ṭīkās often note whether मुनिः is taken as an appositive or a direct address, and how धीमान् is distributed across the epithets.

Here’s a distilled summary of Rāghavānanda’s gloss on that opening verse in his ṭīkā on the Gautamīya Mahātantra, ch. 1, v. 1):  

1. सिद्धाश्रमे  
   – Rāghavānanda points out this isn’t just “a hermitage” but the siddhāśrama of perfected yogins (“siddhas”), where every hall, every tree, even the air is charged with siddhi-śakti.  

2. वसन्  
   – He reads it as “continuously abiding,” not merely “dwelling.”  वसन् here implies unbroken tapas and svādhyāya in that sanctified space.  

3. धीमान्  
   – More than “wise,” Rāghavānanda glosses धीमान् as “one whose wisdom shines forth,” evoking the Vedic dhī-loka (“realm of intellect”)—a superior discrimination that pierces maya.  

4. गौतमो  
   – A patronymic: “he of the Gautama family.”  This ties the scripture back to the ancient Gotama lineage—so that the very name of the text (Gautamīya) flags its authentic guru-paramparā.  

5. भगवन्  
   – He stresses this is nominative, not vocative: “the revered one,” not “O Lord.”  In tantric parlance भगवन् often applies to a perfected sage as much as to Viṣṇu.  

6. मुनिः  
   – “The silent one.”  Rāghavānanda contrasts muniḥ with ṛṣi: the muni’s power lies in inner stillness and direct perception, not in audible teaching.  

—  

Appositional chain  
All four elements—“wise,” “Gautama,” “the revered one,” “the silent sage”—are piled up to describe one and the same person: the guru-founder Suni (called “Muni”).  No extra copula is needed: “In the siddha-ashrama abides that luminous Gautama, the venerable muni.”

Rāghavānanda’s ṭīkā on v. 1 never geolocates the siddhāśrama as “Badrinath” (Badarikashram). He reads it purely as the archetypal hermitage of perfected siddhas—an inner, yogic realm charged with tapas and vidyā—without tying it to any one Himalayan shrine. That said, many Puranic and later Vaiṣṇava texts do identify the famous “siddha-āśrama” with Badarikashram (modern Badrinath):  
- The Skanda Purāṇa and Bhāgavata commentaries narrate that Nara-Nārāyaṇa, Narada Muni and other siddhas performed eternal tapas there.  
- Adi Śaṅkarācārya’s revival of the temple at “Badarikashram” (Joshimath → Badrinath) cemented its reputation as the premier siddha-āśrama.  

But in Rāghavānanda’s line-by-line gloss, “सिद्धाश्रमे” is left un-localized—he deliberately makes it a timeless, all-India seat of yogic power rather than a specific Uttarakhand pilgrimage site.
